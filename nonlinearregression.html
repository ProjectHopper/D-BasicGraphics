%--------------------------------------------------------------------- \begin{slide}{Non-linear regression} Example: In an experiment to determine the effect of concentrations of chemicals on absorbances the following results were obtained: \begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline % after \\: \hline or \cline{col1-col2} \cline{col3-col4} ... Abs & 0.084 & 0.183 & 0.326 & 0.464 & 0.643 & 0.671 & 0.701 \\ Conc & 0.123 & 0.288 & 0.562 & 0.921 & 1.420 & 1.723 & 2.121 \\ \hline \end{tabular} \end{slide} \begin{slide}{Non-linear regression} \begin{itemize} \item A scatterplot indicates that a linear model may be suitable. \item Model 1: $Abs = \beta_{0} + \beta_{1}Conc$. \item But there is a slight curve in the trend, which indicates that a possible quadratic relationship. \item Model 2: $Abs = \beta_{0} + \beta_{1}Conc + \beta_{2}Conc^2$ \item We will construct a model for each and compare the $R^2$ and adjusted $R^2$ values. \end{itemize} \end{slide} \begin{slide}{Model fitting} We fit a linear model and a quadratic model. We need to create a new variable, `conc.sq`' which is simply the square of the concentrations. \begin{verbatim} >Abs<-c(0.084, 0.183, 0.326, 0.464, 0.643 ,0.671,0.701) >Conc<-c(0.123, 0.288, 0.562, 0.921, 1.420,1.723,2.121) > >plot(Conc,Abs) >fit1 = lm(Abs~Conc) > >Conc.sq = Conc^2 >fit2 = lm(Abs~Conc + Conc.sq) \end{verbatim} \end{slide} \begin{slide}{Quadratic model} \begin{verbatim} Call: lm(formula = Abs ~ Conc + Conc.sq) ..... Coefficients: Estimate Std. Error t value Pr(>|t|) (Int) 0.004891 0.011649 0.42 0.696186 Conc 0.655907 0.027240 24.08 1.76e-05 *** Conc.sq -0.154218 0.012072 -12.78 0.000216 *** ...... Multiple R-squared: 0.9985, Adjusted R-squared: 0.9977 ...... \end{verbatim} Validates use of quadratic terms. \end{slide} \begin{slide}{Comparing R square values} \begin{verbatim} > summary(fit1)$r.squared [1] 0.936624 > summary(fit1)$adj.r.squared [1] 0.9239488 > summary(fit2)$adj.r.squared [1] 0.9977258 > summary(fit2)$r.squared [1] 0.9984839 \end{verbatim} Clearly the second model is better. \end{slide} \begin{slide}{Non linear models} \begin{itemize} \item When a curve is evident in the data, can try using quadratic, cubic etc terms. \item Can also try logs, exponentials etc. \end{itemize} \end{slide}
